Socrates: The Unexamined Life

By John Doe | April 2, 2025

Socrates, the ancient Athenian thinker, left no writings of his own. Yet, through the dialogues of Plato[1] and the accounts of Xenophon[2], he emerges as a figure who reshaped philosophy by questioning everything—and everyone.

The Socratic Method

At the heart of Socratic philosophy lies elenchus, a method of cooperative argumentative dialogue. Through questions, Socrates led his interlocutors to expose contradictions in their beliefs, pushing them toward deeper understanding—or humble ignorance.

Ethics and the Soul

For Socrates, virtue was knowledge. He believed that no one does wrong willingly, and that the key to a good life is the health of the soul, achieved through critical self-reflection and pursuit of wisdom.

The Trial and Death

Accused of corrupting the youth and impiety, Socrates was sentenced to death in 399 BCE. He accepted his fate with calmness, insisting that a true philosopher should not fear death[3].

“The unexamined life is not worth living.”

A challenge that echoes through time, urging us to seek meaning through inquiry and integrity.

References

  1. Plato – Student of Socrates who recorded many of his dialogues and teachings.
  2. Xenophon – Historian and contemporary of Socrates who also documented his life.
  3. Apology – One of Plato’s most famous works detailing Socrates' trial and defense.
